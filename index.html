Ever thought about where your water comes from lately?

Throughout 2023, Southern Alberta and the traditional territories of the Blackfoot Confederacy have been hit with extreme drought conditions, which continue to affect us today.

But how did we get here? What climate change phenomena has lead us to this point, in which 15 municipalities have declared agricultural disasters? And what does this growing trend mean for us in the future?

Water

Welcome to Southern Alberta/Treaty 7!

Before we begin, let's take a look at how water travels throughout the province.

Come winter time, large amounts of snow collect in specific areas on the Eastern Slope. These areas are referred to as snow pillows.

As this snow melts slowly throughout the spring and summer, it replenishes several reservoirs throughout Alberta before the irrigation seasons begin.

Rain in June and July helps replenish the reservoirs further, creating a large and healthy source of water. This water is sequestered by municipalities and irrigation districts alike, while also leaving enough water to flow to Saskatchewan - and further to the Hudson's Bay

As the earth warms due to climate change, the snow pack tends to melt earlier and faster. The eastern slopes are critical for water supply in the province. Roughly 90% of water in southwest Alberta comes from snow melt

So what happened this year?

Let's check out the timeline.

January - March 2023

The Eastern Slopes recieve an unusually low snowpack.

April - May 2023

An early warming trend causes the snowpack to feed into the rivers earlier and faster than usual.

June - July 2023

(the most important months for rain)

Southern Alberta recieves little to no rain.

The lack of precipitation leads to higher demand from farmers and more irrigation, which further exacerbates the issue.
